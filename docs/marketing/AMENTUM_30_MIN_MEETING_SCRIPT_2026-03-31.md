# Amentum 30-Minute Meeting Script

Date: 2026-03-31  
Audience: Amentum contracting / partner discussion  
Target meeting length: 30 minutes  
Goal: earn a scoped pilot or technical evaluation

## Core Positioning

Do **not** pitch Helios as a full contested logistics platform.

Pitch it as:

> Helios helps Amentum understand whether the supplier ecosystem behind a contested sustainment solution is trustworthy, resilient, and free of hidden control, cyber, export, and dependency risk.

That is the credible wedge.

## Meeting Objective

By the end of 30 minutes, the goal is to get one of these:

- agreement to a follow-on technical session
- agreement to review a pilot scope
- agreement to identify one mission-thread use case for evaluation

Not:

- a giant platform debate
- a procurement miracle
- a promise that Helios replaces logistics systems

## Three Things To Prove

1. Helios is real, not a concept deck
2. Helios fits a contested sustainment problem Amentum actually has
3. the first pilot can be small, fast, and low-friction

## Talk Track

### Minute 0 to 3: Open

Say:

“Helios is an intelligence and decision-support layer for supplier and dependency risk. We built it to answer a practical problem: before you trust a mission-support or sustainment concept, can you trust the supplier ecosystem underneath it?”

Then:

“For contested sustainment, that means we focus on hidden ownership, control, cyber, export, service, and dependency risks that usually do not show up clearly in a normal vendor review.”

### Minute 3 to 7: Why this matters to Amentum

Say:

“With the new Center for Contested Logistics, the challenge is not only moving things. It is understanding where the sustainment chain is brittle before you operationalize a concept.”

Then say:

“If one supplier, service provider, financing path, or hidden control relationship creates a weak link, that becomes a mission problem. Helios is built to expose that early.”

### Minute 7 to 12: What Helios actually does

Say:

“Helios ingests public and official signals, resolves them into a knowledge graph, and produces supplier passports, dependency graphs, and evidence-backed dossiers.”

Then make the wedge explicit:

“Today our strongest fit is supplier intelligence and dependency-risk visibility for contested sustainment. We are not claiming to be route planning, dispatch, or ERP.”

### Minute 12 to 18: Show one real case

Use one clean proof case only.

Best current options:

- Columbia Helicopters
- Amentum, if final-ready packet is clean enough

Narrate in this order:

1. identity and official corroboration
2. control path
3. intermediary or service dependency signals
4. export or cyber caveats
5. final recommendation and next actions

Say:

“What matters here is not just the score. It is the evidence trail and the hidden dependency picture.”

### Minute 18 to 23: Connect to contested sustainment

Say:

“If you apply this to a sustainment thread instead of one company, Helios can show which suppliers are truly critical, where there are single points of failure, and which nodes need deeper scrutiny before they become operational bottlenecks.”

Then:

“That is the first contested-logistics use case we think is commercially and technically credible.”

### Minute 23 to 27: Pilot proposal

Say:

“We think the right first step is a small pilot around one sustainment thread, not a giant platform deployment.”

Propose:

- 25 to 50 suppliers
- one mission thread
- 30 to 45 days
- outputs:
  - supplier passports
  - dependency graph
  - brittle-node list
  - weekly deltas
  - final briefing packet

### Minute 27 to 30: Close

Ask:

“Would it be useful to scope one contested sustainment use case where we could test this against your current supplier set and show where the hidden fragility actually is?”

Then:

“If that is interesting, the next step on our side would be a technical scoping session and a draft pilot statement of work.”

## What Not To Say

Do not say:

- “We solve contested logistics.”
- “We replace logistics planning tools.”
- “We can already model the whole theater sustainment picture.”
- “We do route optimization.”
- “We have perfect dependency coverage.”

Those claims are not needed and they weaken credibility.

## Questions They Are Likely To Ask

### 1. How is this different from normal vendor screening?

Answer:

“Normal screening usually stops at sanctions, company basics, and a static review. Helios goes deeper into control paths, service dependencies, cyber/export context, and how those risks affect an operationally meaningful supplier set.”

### 2. Is this just OSINT?

Answer:

“It starts with public and official evidence, but the value is in how we resolve and structure it into a usable dependency and decision model. It is not just a search result list.”

### 3. Does this require sensitive data up front?

Answer:

“No. We can start with open-source and customer-provided supplier lists. If there is value, we can discuss deeper integrations later.”

### 4. Are you a contested logistics platform?

Answer:

“Not in the full execution-system sense. The right way to think about Helios today is as the intelligence and resilience layer that helps you trust the sustainment ecosystem behind a contested logistics mission.”

### 5. What would a pilot look like?

Answer:

“One mission thread, 25 to 50 suppliers, 30 to 45 days, evidence-backed outputs, and a final readout on brittle nodes and hidden dependencies.”

## Demo Rules

- use one case, not five
- do not show every tab
- lead with passport, graph, and narrative
- do not linger on unfinished surfaces
- if AI is warming, say so directly and use the final-ready packet when available

## Leave-Behind Structure

If they ask for a follow-up package, send:

1. one-page capability brief
2. one sample passport or dossier
3. one-page pilot scope

## Brutal Read

The meeting will go well if you make Helios feel:

- sharp
- narrow
- credible
- useful immediately

It will go badly if you try to make it sound bigger than it is.
